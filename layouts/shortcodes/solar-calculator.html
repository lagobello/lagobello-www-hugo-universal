Why latitude and seasons change solar production

The Earth is tilted by about 23.44°. During South Texas summer the Northern Hemisphere leans toward the Sun, so the Sun climbs higher and daylight lasts longer. During winter the Sun is lower and the same beam is spread over more ground.

\( \delta_N = 23.44^\circ \sin\left(\frac{2\pi(N-80)}{365}\right) \)
\( \cos \theta_z = \sin\phi\sin\delta + \cos\phi\cos\delta\cos\omega \)
\( E_d = H_d \times A_\text{panels} \times \eta \times PR \)

Here \(N\) is day of year, \(\phi\) is latitude, \(\delta\) is solar declination, \(H_d\) is daily irradiation in kWh/m²/day, \(\eta\) is panel efficiency, and \(PR\) is a practical performance ratio for wiring, inverter, heat, dust, and orientation losses.
